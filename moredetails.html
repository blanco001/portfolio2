Exploring the Mesosphere

Unveiling the Mesosphere: Earth's Middle Layer

What is the Mesosphere?

The mesosphere (/ˈmɛsəˌsfɪər, ˈmɛz-, ˈmiːsə-, -zə-/) is Earth's third atmospheric layer, situated directly above the stratosphere and below the thermosphere. A defining characteristic of the mesosphere is the steady decrease in temperature with increasing altitude.

The mesosphere's boundaries are determined by the stratopause (top of the stratosphere) and the mesopause (coldest part of the atmosphere). These boundaries can vary depending on location and season, with higher boundaries in winter and the tropics, and lower boundaries in summer and at the poles. Generally, the lower boundary ranges from 47 to 51 km (29 to 32 mi), and the upper boundary (mesopause) ranges from 85 to 100 km (53 to 62 mi).

The Middle Atmosphere

The mesosphere and the layer below it, the stratosphere, are sometimes referred to collectively as the "middle atmosphere." This region spans roughly between 12 and 80 km above Earth's surface.

The Mesopause and Beyond

The mesopause, located at an altitude of 80-90 km, separates the mesosphere from the thermosphere. Interestingly, the mesopause nearly coincides with the turbopause. The turbopause is the region where different gases in the atmosphere are well-mixed due to turbulent eddies. Above this level, the atmosphere becomes less uniform because the scale heights of different gases vary based on their molecular masses.

Near Space: A Fuzzy Boundary

The term "near space" is sometimes used to describe regions within the mesosphere. It lacks a precise technical definition but typically refers to the area roughly between the Armstrong Limit (19 km) and the Kármán Line (where aerodynamics give way to astrodynamics for flight). Alternatively, it can refer to the space between the highest commercial airline flight altitude (12.2 km) and the lowest perigee of orbiting satellites (73 km).
